## Fiction

- Fictional stories about original characters, settings, and events, using plot and character arcs to tell a cohesive narrative.
- A broad grouping based on themes and storytelling conventions, setting reader expectations, and aiding discovery:

### Action and Thriller

- Fast-paced stories driven by danger, conflict, and high-stakes scenarios. These stories emphasize adrenaline-filled sequences, life-or-death decisions, and psychological tension.

### Culture and Identity

- Stories that explore cultural backgrounds, heritage, and personal or collective identity. Often reflective or rooted in community experiences, social roles, or diasporic narratives.

### Fantasy

- Stories that include magical, mythical, or otherworldly elements. Set in imagined worlds with heroes, magic, quests, and supernatural beings.

### Historical

- Narratives set in a specific historical period, often focusing on historical events, figures, or settings.

### Horror

- Stories designed to frighten, unsettle, or explore the darker aspects of the human psyche. Often includes supernatural, psychological, or gruesome elements.

### LGBTQ+

- Stories centered on queer characters and experiences, exploring gender, sexuality, identity, and relationships.

### Mystery

- Plots driven by the unraveling of secrets, crimes, or unknowns. Focuses on investigation, deduction, and suspense.

### Paranormal

- Stories set in the real world that involve supernatural phenomena, creatures, or ghosts. These stories blend everyday settings with eerie or otherworldly elements.

### Romance

- Stories that center on romantic relationships, exploring emotional connection, attraction, tension, and growth between characters.

### Science Fiction

- Explores futuristic, speculative, or technologically advanced concepts. These stories imagine what could be possible through science, innovation, or the disruption of known physical laws.

### Social Themes

- Fiction with a strong focus on social commentary, justice, activism, and real-world issues like mental health, discrimination, and religion.

## Fanfiction

- Fictional works written by fans who create their own content using pre-existing stories, celebrities, and well-known characters:

### Fandoms

- The specific subject, media property, or interest around which the story is focused.

### Fandom Topic

- The central media, subject, or figure that a fanfiction is based on. Multiple topics can apply to a single story, reflecting the range of source inspirations.

### Fanfiction Format

- The narrative structure or stylistic approach used in fanfiction, often reflecting how a story engages with or diverges from canon. These formats help set expectations for tone, setting, perspective, or creative intent.

## Non-Fiction

- Writing based on real experiences, facts, or commentary, encompassing personal reflections, informational content, recommendations, critiques, discussion, contests, and updates. Non-fiction categories include:

### Autobiography

- A story about the writer's life in their own words.

### Awards & Contests

- Stories to explain the rules and information for contests and awards

### Book Club

- Stories for discussing other stories on Wattpad.

### Informational and Guide

- Non-fiction writing that provides information or advice

### Interviews

- Interviews with writers discussing their work and lives.

### Journal / Diary

- Non-narrative writing detailing the writer's day or internal thoughts and feelings.

### Recommendations

- Suggestions of things to read, watch, or listen to.

### Reflection

- Non-narrative writing focused on a past event or topic, exploring and examining what occurred.

### Reviews

- Stories for reviewing and critiquing other stories on Wattpad.

### Updates

- Communications from a writer to their readers that provide new information, such as release dates or hiatuses.

### Workshop and Feedback

- Stories centered on providing feedback and help with writing, editing, and cover creation.

## Poetry

- Literary expression through rhythm, imagery, and carefully chosen language to express emotions, ideas, or moments. Poetry categories include:

### Collection

- A grouping of more than one poem, often exploring a theme.

### Epic

- A longform narrative poem, often telling the story of heroic deeds (such as The Iliad or The Odyssey)

### Free Verse

- A poem that does not follow a specified form, structure, or rhyming scheme.

### Haiku

- A poem written in three lines of 5 syllables, 7 syllables, 5 syllables for a total of seventeen, originating in Japan.

### Limerick

- An often humorous poem in which the longer first, second, and fifth lines rhyme with each other (A) and the shorter third and fourth lines rhyme with each other (B), for a rhyming pattern of AABBA.

### Lyrics / Lyrical

- A poem expressing emotion or feeling, or the literal lyrics of a song.

### Prose / Narrative

- A story presented in metered verse (like The Canterbury Tales)

### Sonnet

- A poem of fourteen lines set to a rhyming scheme, often expressing an emotion.

## Type

- The broadest classification used to define the nature of a story’s content and its intended audience. Format captures the fundamental structure and creative intent of the work.
